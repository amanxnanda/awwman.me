The day I lost my kidney



The 17th of August, 2023, had started like any dull, mundane day. I went to the gym, cooked and ate breakfast and then went to the office. But I had a little extra bounce to my steps because I was supposed to go to Delhi back home in the second half of the day. This was an impromptu trip back home; the tickets were booked just 40 hours before the flight. So, I worked until lunchtime and ate my last meal in Bangalore with other team members. I made fake promises to return early, bid my goodbyes, and left.

I hadn’t finished the packing, so I plucked some clothes from my closet, rolled them up and stuffed them into a bag. By this time, I was too late to go by bus, so Bangalore’s infamous cab ride to the Airport was the only option left, which can burn a hole in your pocket so deep that even Bruce Wayne from the Dark Knight trilogy without ropes can’t climb. I reached the airport on time, but time wasn’t the only thing I wanted to save. An hour later, I found myself in the middle seat of Vistara, where on the right, a cute little baby girl was not letting go of my shiny kadda (bracelet) while mumbling something to her grandfather and on the right was your typical boss-lady. This lady tried to squeeze in one last call to her subordinate to inform “how not to fuck this client up”. After a while, I started watching “Before Sunset,” but ironically, I couldn’t finish it before sunset. The movie and sunset setting on the west were both beautiful. By the time the sun was nowhere to be found, I had landed in Delhi.

To protest against Bangalore’s last-mile infrastructure, I decided to go by Delhi metro so later I could rub it to Bangalore (which is impossible because it won't understand unless I speak in Kannada) to see how great Delhi is. I had been travelling in the Delhi metro before I knew the meaning of my name, which was eventually told by an elderly person when I was 6 or 7 in the metro. But my stupid ass still gets nervous, and why would it not be? There are many lines of different colours: red, blue, green, pink, purple, magenta and whatnot; I bet even the LGBTQIA+ flag doesn’t have that many colours. While leaving the airport, I said, “It’s easy; just go to the orange line, then yellow, and finally blue, and you’ll be home before dinner”. Orange, yellow and blue; only if it was that simple.

I reached the orange line before I could finish the few top singles by seedhe maut, and after a while, I felt more of a Punjabi vibe. At 9:55 pm, I’m at the New Delhi metro station (orange line) waiting for the metro, which will take me to Rajiv Chowk (Yellow line), just one station away. I see the train coming. I grab my suitcase with my left hand, change the current track to Rubicon Drill, put my phone in my right pocket, and then prepare myself to wrestle to get onto the metro. The gate opens, I shove myself in, and I feel something on my right, which I knew right away what it was but refused to believe for a second. I did my usual pat, and it was clear as day what had happened. I was robbed of my most-priced possession, my constant in the sine graph of life; the only things closer to me were probably my intrusive thoughts. My beautiful purple iPhone 14. It was covered with a black matt cover, and a skin with a colourful geometric design was peeping through everywhere. It embodied me, and there couldn’t be a better representation of myself. People who know me know what I’m talking about. I had especially bought iPhone 14 so it's easier for people to call me “iPhone ka Chuddah (14)”, which translates to “iPhone’s fuck”. All of it was long gone now.

What happened right after the incident is just sad, which involved people without spine or humility and screwed up the state of our system. Jo ho gaya so ho gaya (Whatever has happened, has happened), and nothing can be done about it now. I pulled myself together and took the saddest auto-rickshaw ride of my life to home. With a heavy heart and lighter pockets, I entered the house with a long day gone past me but a longer face with me.

End.
